01

What is the SAT EBRW?

The SAT Reading & Writing section (formerly called EBRW — Evidence-Based Reading and Writing) tests how well students can read, analyze, and edit written English. Unlike the old paper SAT, the current Digital SAT presents one short passage per question, making it faster-paced and more precise. The good news: it is highly learnable and highly pattern-driven.

800 Maximum score for the Reading & Writing section
54 Total questions across both Reading & Writing modules
64 Minutes total to complete the R&W section

Why It Matters

  • Colleges use SAT scores for admissions decisions
  • Many scholarships have SAT score thresholds
  • R&W score reflects college readiness in language
  • Improvement here is very achievable with strategy

The Digital Shift

  • Moved from paper to digital in 2024
  • Adaptive: Module 2 difficulty adjusts to Module 1 performance
  • Short passages — one per question (25–150 words)
  • Multiple choice only — 4 answer options per question
02

Test Structure & Format

Section Overview

  • 2 Modules of Reading & Writing
  • 27 questions per module
  • 32 minutes per module
  • ~71 seconds per question on average
  • 4 answer choices per question (A–D)
  • No penalty for wrong answers — always guess!

Adaptive Testing Logic

  • Module 1 is the same difficulty for everyone
  • Module 2 adapts based on Module 1 performance
  • Better Module 1 → harder (higher-scoring) Module 2
  • Worse Module 1 → easier Module 2, lower score ceiling
  • Goal: always aim to perform well in Module 1

Passage Format

Each question comes with its own short passage (25–150 words). Passages are drawn from: Literature, History & Social Studies, Humanities, and Science. Some questions include tables, charts, or graphs paired with the passage. Students do not need prior subject knowledge — everything needed to answer is in the passage.

⭑ Key Insight

Because passages are short and self-contained, strong test-takers don't need to be great "readers" in the traditional sense — they need to be great at extracting precise meaning quickly. This is a skill that can be taught and drilled.

03

Question Types Breakdown

The SAT Reading & Writing section has four main skill categories. Every question on the test maps to one of these types.

Category A

Information & Ideas

Tests whether students can understand, interpret, and use information from passages and paired visuals. Covers main ideas, details, inferences, and data analysis.

Sub-types: Central Ideas · Details · Inferences · Command of Evidence (Textual & Quantitative)
Category B

Craft & Structure

Tests ability to analyze how authors write — word choice, text structure, purpose, and perspective. Also tests cross-text comparisons between two short excerpts.

Sub-types: Words in Context · Text Structure & Purpose · Cross-Text Connections
Category C

Expression of Ideas

Tests editing and revision skills — how to improve clarity, add relevant information, use transitions logically, and synthesize notes into a coherent sentence.

Sub-types: Rhetorical Synthesis · Transitions
Category D

Standard English Conventions

Tests grammar and usage rules: punctuation, sentence boundaries, subject-verb agreement, verb tense, pronoun clarity, modifiers, and more.

Sub-types: Boundaries · Form, Structure & Sense
⭑ Distribution

Roughly: Information & Ideas (~26%) · Craft & Structure (~28%) · Expression of Ideas (~20%) · Standard English Conventions (~26%). Grammar questions tend to feel easier to improve quickly, making them a great starting focus for most students.

04

Core Strategy Principles

1

Read the Question First

Always read the question before reading the passage. Knowing what you're looking for makes reading faster and more focused. The SAT passage is short — you can afford to read it twice if needed, but read the question first.

2

Eliminate Wrong Answers, Don't Just Pick Right Ones

SAT answer choices are carefully written. Wrong answers often sound plausible — they reference the passage but distort the meaning. Train yourself to cross out wrong choices rather than just guessing what "sounds good."

3

The Correct Answer Must Be Provable From the Passage

For reading questions, the right answer will always be directly supported by text in the passage. If you can't point to specific words that back up your answer, it's probably wrong. No outside knowledge needed.

4

Grammar Rules Are Consistent — Learn Them Once

Standard English Convention questions test the same rules every time. Punctuation, subject-verb agreement, sentence boundaries — these follow rigid patterns. Learn the rules and you gain easy, repeatable points.

5

Never Leave a Question Blank

There is no penalty for wrong answers on the SAT. If you run out of time or are truly stuck, always guess. Statistically, random guessing gives you a 25% chance of getting it right.
