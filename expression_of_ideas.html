Sub-type 1 Rhetorical Synthesis
These are unique to the digital SAT. The student is given a set of bullet-point notes (as if taken by a researcher or student) and must choose the answer that best synthesises those notes to accomplish a specific goal — for example, "to emphasise a contrast" or "to introduce the study to a general audience."

Both the content (what information is included) and the rhetoric (how it's framed) must match the stated goal.
"The student wants to emphasise the unexpected nature of the finding. Which choice most effectively uses relevant information from the notes to accomplish this goal?"
Strategy: First, identify the exact goal in the question. Then eliminate answers that include irrelevant notes or frame the information in a way that doesn't match the goal.
⭑ Key Trap Wrong answers often include accurate information from the notes but miss the rhetorical goal — they summarise instead of emphasising contrast, or describe instead of introducing to a general audience.
Sub-type 2 Transitions
These present two sentences or a sentence with a blank and ask which transition word or phrase best connects the ideas. The relationship between the two ideas determines the right transition — contrast, addition, cause-effect, example, concession, etc.
"Which choice completes the text with the most logical transition?"
Strategy: Before looking at the options, read both sentences and ask yourself — does the second idea agree, disagree, add to, or result from the first? Then pick the transition that matches that relationship.
⭑ Key Trap Many students choose a transition that sounds sophisticated (e.g., "nonetheless") without checking whether the logical relationship actually calls for contrast. Always match the relationship first, then find the word.

Category Strategy

What unites this category

Expression of Ideas tests writing and revision skills. Unlike other categories, students aren't just comprehending — they're evaluating how well a piece of writing achieves its intended effect. The question is always: does this accomplish the goal?

Key habits to build

  • For synthesis: identify the rhetorical goal before reading answers
  • For transitions: name the relationship before looking at options
  • Learn transition categories: contrast, addition, cause, example
  • Avoid overthinking — the goal is always stated explicitly
⭑ Note Expression of Ideas accounts for ~20% of the R&W section — the smallest of the four categories. Rhetorical Synthesis is completely new to the digital SAT format; students who practise it early gain a significant advantage since many haven't encountered this question type before.
