Sub-type 1 Central Ideas & Details
These questions test whether the student can identify what a passage is primarily about (central idea) or locate a specific piece of information explicitly stated in the text (detail).

Central idea questions ask about the whole passage. Detail questions ask about one specific fact, reason, or description.
"Which choice best states the main purpose of the text?"
Strategy: Read the full passage and ask — what is the author's main point? Eliminate answers that are too narrow (only one detail) or too broad.
⭑ Key Trap Wrong answers for central idea questions often pick a true detail from the passage but miss the overall point. The correct answer must cover the whole text.
Sub-type 2 Inferences
These go one step beyond what the passage says. Students must use the passage's information to draw a reasonable logical conclusion — something the author implies but doesn't state outright.
"Based on the text, what can most reasonably be inferred about the researcher's findings?"
Strategy: The correct answer will always be directly supportable from the text. Eliminate answers that go too far or bring in outside assumptions.
⭑ Key Trap The most common trap: an answer that sounds logical from general knowledge but isn't backed by the passage. The passage is always your only source.

Sub-type 3 Command of Evidence — Textual
These come in pairs. The first question asks for a conclusion; the second asks which lines from the passage best support that conclusion. Sometimes both questions are fused into one.

They test whether students can find the specific textual evidence behind an idea — not just guess.
"Which quotation from the text most effectively illustrates the claim made in the previous question?"
Strategy: Work backwards — confirm your answer to Q1 first. Then find the quote that directly proves it, not one that's just nearby or related.
⭑ Key Trap Wrong quotes are often from the same paragraph as the correct one. They sound relevant but prove a slightly different point. Precision is everything here.
Sub-type 4 Command of Evidence — Quantitative
These pair a short passage with a table, chart, or graph. Students must interpret the data and determine whether it supports, undermines, or is unrelated to a claim in the passage.

No math is required — only the ability to read data carefully.
"Which choice most effectively uses data from the table to support the student's claim?"
Strategy: Always read the table's title, axis labels, and units before looking at the data. Many students lose points by misreading what the table actually shows.
⭑ Key Trap A very common wrong answer uses real numbers from the table but applies them to the wrong row, column, or comparison. Read every label carefully.
02

Category Strategy

What unites this category

All four sub-types share one core demand: everything must come from the passage. No outside knowledge, no general logic, no personal opinion. Your job is to read precisely and extract exactly what the text says or implies.

Key habits to build

  • Read the question before the passage
  • Annotate what the passage is doing as you read
  • Eliminate answers that go beyond the text
  • For data questions, read labels before numbers
⭑ Note Information & Ideas questions account for roughly 26% of the R&W section — about 7 questions per module. Strong performance here requires no special vocabulary, just careful reading and disciplined elimination.
